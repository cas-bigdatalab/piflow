ABSTRACT
Autoencoder-based approaches for Unsupervised Anomaly
Detection (UAD) in brain MRI have recently gained a lot
of attention and have shown promising performance. How-
ever, brain MR images are particularly complex and require
large model capacity for learning a proper reconstruction,
which existing methods encounter by restricting themselves
to downsampled data or anatomical subregions. In this work,
we show that models with limited capacity can be trained
and used for UAD in full brain MR images at their native
resolution by introducing skip-connections, a concept which
has already proven beneﬁcial for biomedical image segmen-
tation and image-to-image translation, and a dropout-based
mechanism to prevent the model from learning an identity
mapping. In an ablative study on two different pathologies
we show considerable improvements over State-of-the-Art
Autoencoder-based UAD models. The stochastic nature of
the model also allows to investigate epistemic uncertainty in
our so-called Skip-Autoencoder, which is brieﬂy portrayed.
Index Terms— Anomaly Detection, Skip-Autoencoders,
Uncertainty
1. INTRODUCTION
Deep unsupervised representation learning and deep genera-
tive modeling have recently turned out to be promising tools
for Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) in 2D and 3D
data. Autoencoders (AEs) and Generative models such as
GANs or V AEs, respectively, have been successfully used to
model normal anatomy. Since such models have only seen
normality, anomalies will not properly be reconstructed and
thus can be detected directly in image-space from high recon-
struction errors. In general, UAD in brain MRI is of great
interest for the Medical Image Analysis community, as it re-
lieves from the need for pixel-level annotations—an impor-
tant, but expensive and often scarce prerequisite for super-
vised training of deep neural networks—and allows to detect
arbitrary pathologies without a-priori knowing about their ap-
pearance.
In the medical ﬁeld, numerous works have been devoted
to UAD in different modalities. In initial work, Schlegl et
al. [1] proposed GANs for detecting anomalies in retinal
OCT data, however at a level of small image patches. Baur et
al. [2] introduced a feed-forward solution for UAD in brain
MRI at high resolution with a combination of spatial AEs and
GANs. While this method showed the ability to detect small
MS lesions in high resolution data, it was limited to a narrow
ﬁeld of view centered around the axial midline. Another in-
teresting concept based on bayesian convolutional AEs was
proposed by Pawlowski et al. [3] to detect anomalies in head
CT images, but their approach was again limited to few mid-
axial slices. Zimmerer et al. [4] recently formulated UAD
as an inpainting problem with their context variational AE,
but validated their method at a resolution of only 64× 64px.
Chen et al. [5] proposed constrained adversarial AEs for de-
tecting large lesions in brain MR images, and there have also
been ﬁrst results on UAD in volumetric head CT data using
3D AEs [6]. However, both approaches were also limited
to low resolution, which comes at the cost of information
loss. This might be critical in the presence of small anoma-
lous structures such as MS lesions. We hypothesize that the
restriction to low-resolution and anatomical subregions are
coping mechanisms to trade-off the limited model capacity,
in particularly the dimensionality of the latent space, and the
high complexity of brain MR data. For example, in brain
MRI it is especially important to properly reconstruct ﬁne
details such as gyri und sulci, as they would otherwise falsely
be recognized as anomalies.
Contribution: Motivated by their success in supervised
biomedical image segmentation [7, 8] and image-to-image

troduce a dropout-based identity mitigation mechanism. In an
ablative study involving two different pathologies (Multiple
Sclerosis and Glioblastoma), we show considerable improve-
ments over standard AE architectures in the task of detecting

2. METHODOLOGY
2.1. Modeling healthy anatomy with Skip-Autoencoders
Similar to previous work, we aim to model normal brain
anatomy with an Autoencoder (AE) and detect anomalies
from reconstruction residuals directly in 2D image space. To
achieve this for complex medical data such as MRI without
imposing constraints on resolution or ﬁeld-of-view, we pro-
pose the so-called Skip-Autoencoder (skipAE): We introduce
skip-connections in AEs (see Fig. 1) and train a model fθ(x)
to compress and reconstruct only anomaly-free data x∈X N .
We optimize the model’s parametersθ with the loss
L(x, ˆ x) = ℓ1(x, ˆ x) + gdl(x, ˆ x) (1)
where ℓ1 is the ℓ1-distance between the input x and
its reconstruction ˆ x, and gdl(·,·) is the gradient difference
loss which forces the model to reconstruct image gradients
well [10].
Skip-connections have proven very useful in biomedical
image segmentation [7] and image restoration [11] as they
enhance gradient ﬂow and provide a view on image features
at multiple scales, leveraging both local and global features
in the segmentation process. Their importance has been thor-
oughly studied in [8]. Recently, they have also proven beneﬁ-
cial in GAN-based image-to-image translation [9]. There, the
authors employed a stochastic U-Net like architecture as the
generator and identiﬁed skip-connections as a key component
to obtain high ﬁdelity results.

2.4. Anomaly Detection
To leverage a trained non-bayesian model for UAD, we sim-
ply compute the residuals r = |x− ˆ x| for all axial slices
of a testing subject. For our bayesian Skip-Autoencoder, we
ﬁrst aggregate a predictive mean ofn MC reconstructions be-
fore the residuals are computed. Then, thresholding can be
applied to produce a binary delineation. To reduce False Pos-
itives and to provide the physician with a smooth Precision-
Recall-Curve from which an operating pointt can be chosen,
we further apply 3D bilateral ﬁltering to the volume of resid-
ual images.
3. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
3.1. Dataset
In our experiments we use a non-public dataset containing
FLAIR and T1-weighted MR images of 50 healthy subjects,
49 subjects with MS lesions and 26 subjects with Glioblas-
toma. All data were acquired with a Philips Achieva 3T
MR scanner and annotated by expert Neuro-radiologists. In-
formed consent was waived. In preparation, the T1 images
were used for brain extraction using ROBEX [13] and for
co-registration to bring all subjects and scans to the SRI24
ATLAS [14] space. For our experiments, we then only use
co-registered FLAIR images, normalized into[−1; 1]. We uti-
lize all non-empty axial FLAIR slices of the healthy subjects
to train the models, where non-empty slices are determined
from the brain extraction. From each pathology, we randomly
pick three validation subjects each to determine an Operating
Point (OP) for assessment and use all others for testing.
3.2. Metrics
For evaluation we compute the Precision-Recall-Curve (PRC)
over all testing data and quantitatively compare methods
by their area under this curve (AUPRC) as well as the
patient-wise DICE-Score (determined at the most optimal
OP for the validation samples) across all testing patients.
The AUPRC provides a meaningful measure for delineation
(and thus detection) performance in the presence of heavy
class-imbalance, which is apparent in both pathologies we
experiment with.
3.3. Experiment A: Investigating Dropout
First, we trained three skipAEs with complete skip-connectivity
at different rates of dropout d∈{ 0, 0.1, 0.5} for 150 epochs
to investigate the effect on identity-mitigation and UAD per-
formance. We witness only subtle variation among their
PRCs (see Fig. 3.3c), which implies that an identity mapping
is not learned, regardless of dropout. Apparently, random
weight initialization is enough to prevent identity.
3.4. Experiment B: Exploring Skip-Connections
Afterwards, we trained a variety of skipAEs with different
combinations of skip-connections at ﬁxed d, again for 150
epochs. Since results from experiment A indicate that there
is no necessity for dropout, we set d = 0 .0. As a base-
line, we ﬁrst trained a spatial Autoencoder [2] (sAE) based
on our skipAE architecture with all the skip-connections re-
moved. To that, we compare models with either one, two or
all skip-connections. Further, to determine if lower levels of
the skipAE are not simply bypassed, we trained a model with
skip-connections, but no bottleneck (z and d3 removed, see
Table 1, #2), and a model which purely consists of only the
ﬁrst skip-connection (see Table 1, #6). We also compare to
the skipBAE, i.e. the bayesian variant of our model following
the work in [3]. The skipBAE aggregates a predictive mean
from 100 MC samples at a dropout probability p = 0.1. All
results are reported in Table 1. The best performance on MS
was obtained with a single skip-connection close to the bot-
tleneck (see Table. 1, #3). With increasingly excessive use
of skip-connections, the performance on MS slightly drops,
and slightly increases for Glioblastoma. In comparison to the
sAE, the best skipAE yields an absolute improvement of 55%
in AUPRC for detecting MS lesions and 17% for Glioblas-
toma. While still considerably better than the sAE, removal
of the bottleneck (skipAE w/o z) leads to a reduction of per-
formance in all measures for all pathologies. Interestingly,
the skipBAE does not perform as good as the non-bayesian
model.

3.6. Discussion
Our ablative study shows that skip-connections in AEs are
very helpful for UAD, in particular for the detection of hyper-
intense anomalies such as MS lesions. Our skipAE outper-
forms the spatial AE by a large margin on both pathologies.
At the same time, we also show that sacriﬁcing the bottleneck
or any lower layers of the skipAE impairs UAD performance,
which implies that it beneﬁts heavily from the multi-scale fea-
ture extraction. The number of skip-connections needs to be
carefully selected, as their excessive use enables the recon-
struction of anomalies as well, though. On hyperintense le-
sions, we ﬁnd the skipBAE to be inferior to the skipAE, be-
cause averaging over the MC samples induces slight blurri-
ness in the reconstructions. Unfortunately, the AE-based de-
tection of Glioblastoma does not work as good as the detec-
tion of MS lesions. Glioblastoma are morphologically and
intensity-wise much more diverse than purely hyperintense
lesions and thus beneﬁt from more skip-connections, but the
non-hyperintense tumor areas are often reconstructed too well
to be recognized from the residuals.
4. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, we showed that skip-connections in AEs lead
to considerable improvements in hyperintense anomaly de-
tection in brain MR data. The skip-connections enable high
ﬁdelity reconstructions, which are key for UAD in brain MRI.
At no time, an identity mapping was learned, even if dropout
was disabled. However, if dropout is active, the resulting
model allows to estimate epistemic uncertainty. Our brief ex-
cursus to epistemic uncertainty revealed that residuals vary
mainly for healthy tissue, and hyperintense anomalous areas
tend to have low uncertainty. We leave more thorough inves-
tigations of the role of uncertainty in UAD to future work,
though.
